# Treating Toxin Contamination in Shrimp Tanks

One of the most devastating situations for a shrimp keeper is finding mass casualties after someone sprays insecticide nearby, uses perfume, or applies cleaning chemicals close to the tank. Environmental toxins are the *most silent* cause of shrimp death — there are no clear warning signs, and once shrimp start dying, it happens extremely fast.

This guide will walk you through how to identify, respond to, and prevent toxin contamination in your shrimp tank.

---

## Common Types of Toxins

### Group 1 — Airborne / Aerosol Chemicals

| Source | Danger Level | Speed of Harm |
|--------|-------------|---------------|
| Mosquito spray (Raid, Baygon...) | ⚠️⚠️⚠️ Very High | 15–60 minutes |
| Ant/roach spray insecticides | ⚠️⚠️⚠️ Very High | 15–60 minutes |
| Perfume, air freshener, deodorant spray | ⚠️⚠️ High | 1–4 hours |
| Paint, varnish, nail polish | ⚠️⚠️⚠️ Very High | 30 min–2 hours |
| Passive cigarette smoke | ⚠️ Moderate | Accumulates over hours |

### Group 2 — Contact Chemicals (via hands or equipment)

- **Soap, hand cream, lotion**: Residue on hands introduced into the tank
- **Cleaning chemicals on equipment** (buckets, silicon tubing, heaters): Incompletely rinsed
- **Fish medication** (copper sulfate CuSO₄, formalin): Uncontrolled dosage
- **Plant fertilizers** containing high doses of copper or iron
- **Tap water** with elevated chloramine (during utility top-up days)

### Group 3 — Secondary Toxins from Within the Tank

- Suddenly decaying plants causing an ammonia spike
- Uneaten food left for extended periods
- Anaerobic substrate releasing H₂S gas (rotten egg smell)

## Recognizing Signs of Contamination

### Acute symptoms (within hours)

- 🚨 **Shrimp swimming to the surface** repeatedly, gasping at the waterline
- 🚨 **Shrimp lying on their sides** at the bottom, weakly moving
- 🚨 **Shrimp curling their legs**, abdomen contracting abnormally
- 🚨 **Mass deaths** — multiple shrimp dying simultaneously
- 🚨 **Sudden bleaching of color** across the entire colony

### Sub-acute symptoms (hours to 1–2 days)

- Shrimp stop eating, hiding continuously without coming out
- Berried females suddenly dropping eggs
- Shrimp attempting to molt but unable to escape (dying in the molt)
- Sporadic individual deaths stretched over several days

### Distinguishing from other causes

| Symptom | Toxin Contamination | Water Parameter Shock | Bacterial Disease |
|---------|--------------------|-----------------------|-------------------|
| Speed of deaths | Very fast | Fast | Gradual |
| Shrimp surfacing | Many, sudden | Possible | Rarely |
| Spreads progressively | No | No | Yes |
| Associated with chemicals | Yes | No | No |

## Emergency Response Upon Discovery

### Step 1 — Act within the first 5 minutes

```
✅ TURN OFF the filter immediately if it's pulling contaminated water through
✅ NET OUT all surviving shrimp into a bucket of clean, dechlorinated water
✅ DO NOT add any other chemicals to the tank right now
```

> **Important note**: When moving shrimp to a temporary bucket, use water from *another tank* or RO/tap water that has been dechlorinated, at the same temperature. Never use straight tap water.

### Step 2 — Emergency water change (first 30 minutes)

If you cannot net all shrimp out quickly or the tank is too large:

1. **Change 30–40% immediately** with pre-prepared water matching the tank parameters
2. **Run maximum aeration** to oxidize and drive off volatile chemicals
3. **Run activated carbon** immediately — this is the single most important step

### Step 3 — Targeted treatment by toxin type

#### 🔹 Mosquito spray / insecticide contamination

This is the **most dangerous** case because pyrethrin/pyrethroid (active ingredients in Raid, Baygon) are extraordinarily toxic to crustaceans.

| Method | How to Apply | Effectiveness |
|--------|-------------|--------------|
| **Activated carbon** | 50–100g per 100L, run in filter for 48–72 hours | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ |
| **Sponge filter (Easy-Filter)** | Max flow rate, add beneficial bacteria | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ |
| **Continuous water changes** | 20–30% every 6–8 hours for 2 days | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ |
| **Na₂S₂O₃ (sodium thiosulfate)** | Neutralizes chloramine — does NOT treat pyrethroid | ⭐⭐ |
| **Zeolite** | Absorbs ammonia from dead shrimp | ⭐⭐⭐ |

> ⚠️ **Reality check**: If mosquito spray was applied directly into or very near the tank (in a small enclosed room), survival rates are very low even with prompt treatment. Prioritize saving survivors and prepare to reset the tank.

#### 🔹 Perfume / air freshener / deodorizer contamination

- Change **50%** of water immediately
- Run activated carbon for 24–48 hours
- Strong aeration for 24 continuous hours
- Usually less lethal than insecticides if exposure was limited

#### 🔹 Soap / cleaning product contamination

- Change **70–80%** of water or do a complete tank reset
- Do not rely on activated carbon alone — water changes are primary
- Check pH and TDS after water changes

#### 🔹 Contamination via human hands

- Change **20–30%** of water
- Run activated carbon for 24 hours
- Usually self-recovering if treated early

## The Sponge Filter (Easy-Filter) — Its Role in Toxin Treatment

A sponge filter is an **indispensable tool** when treating a contaminated tank — not because it filters chemicals, but because of what it does for the tank's biology:

### Why sponge filters are effective in this situation

1. **Continuous oxygen supply**: Toxins impair shrimp's ability to absorb oxygen — aeration from the sponge filter helps them breathe

2. **Preserve beneficial bacteria surface area**: The bacteria colony in the sponge filter breaks down ammonia from dead shrimp, preventing a secondary ammonia spike

3. **Completely safe**: No risk of shrimp being sucked into the filter intake — weakened shrimp are extremely vulnerable to HOB and canister filter intakes

4. **Bacteria survive multiple water changes**: When performing repeated water changes, the bacteria colony in the sponge is fully preserved

### Optimizing your sponge filter during emergency treatment

```
• Set air pump to maximum flow
• Dose additional beneficial bacteria directly onto the sponge (SL-Aqua, ADA Bacter)
• Use two sponge filters if available (more bacteria = better)
• Add a bag of activated carbon in the flow path next to the sponge
```

## Proper Water Change Protocol During Treatment

### Principles for water changes in a contaminated tank

Water changes are the most direct treatment, but **must be done correctly** to avoid compounding stress on already weakened shrimp:

| Principle | Reason |
|-----------|--------|
| New water temperature = tank temperature ±1°C | Prevents temperature shock when shrimp are already stressed |
| New water TDS ≈ current tank TDS | Prevents osmotic shock |
| Pour slowly, never pour forcefully | Prevents stirring substrate and releasing H₂S |
| Use dechlorinated water only | Chloramine is more toxic than the original contamination |

### 5-day emergency treatment water change schedule

| Day | Volume Changed | Supporting Actions |
|-----|---------------|-------------------|
| Day 1 (discovery) | 30–40% × 2 times | Activated carbon + maximum aeration |
| Day 2 | 20–30% | Continue activated carbon |
| Day 3 | 20% | Test ammonia/nitrite levels |
| Day 4 | 15% | Observe shrimp behavior |
| Day 5 | 10% | Return to normal maintenance schedule |

> 💡 After 5 days, if shrimp are behaving normally with no further deaths — your tank has passed the critical window.

## When to Do a Complete Tank Reset

Sometimes trying to save a contaminated tank causes more losses. Consider a **complete reset** if:

- ❌ More than 50% of your colony died within 24 hours
- ❌ Spray was applied directly into the tank or in a small enclosed room (< 10m²)
- ❌ Concentrated cleaning chemicals entered the tank (bleach, detergent...)
- ❌ Shrimp continue dying after 2 days of active treatment

### Safe complete tank reset procedure

1. Move all surviving shrimp into a temporary bucket/tank with clean water
2. Remove all equipment, soak in 1% salt solution for 30 minutes, rinse thoroughly
3. Drain the tank completely and scrub the glass — **no soap** — hot water and salt only
4. Keep the sponge filter submerged in the temporary shrimp bucket to preserve bacteria
5. Refill the tank with correctly-parameterized water, re-establish cycling for 24–48h before returning shrimp

## Prevention — More Important Than Treatment

### Golden rules around a shrimp tank

✅ **Always keep the lid closed** — especially when chemicals are being used nearby  
✅ **Inform all household members**: Do not spray chemicals in rooms with shrimp tanks  
✅ **Rinse hands with plain cold water** (no soap) before putting hands in the tank  
✅ **Soak new equipment** in old tank water for 24 hours before first use  
✅ **Never share equipment** between fish tanks and shrimp tanks  

### Safe distances and precautions

| Activity | Safe Distance | Additional Steps |
|----------|--------------|-----------------|
| Indoor mosquito spray | Go outside, close the tank room door | Wait 2–4 hours, ventilate thoroughly before re-entry |
| Painting walls | Different room, well ventilated | Cover tank tightly with plastic wrap |
| Burning incense/candles | > 2 meters away | Good ventilation |
| Essential oil diffuser | > 1.5 meters away | Direct airflow away from the tank |

### Emergency supplies to always keep on hand

```
📦 Shrimp tank "first aid kit":
• Activated carbon: 200–500g
• Na₂S₂O₃ (dechlorinator) / ShrimpSafe
• Backup sponge filter with established bacteria
• Clean 20L bucket for temporary housing
• Ammonia and nitrite test kits
• 20L of pre-prepared RO or dechlorinated water
```

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Someone sprayed insecticide in another room. Could my tank be affected?**  
A: Possibly, if air circulates between rooms. Keep the tank lid closed and turn off fans blowing toward the tank during spraying.

**Q: All my shrimp died. Can I reuse the old tank water?**  
A: Not recommended. Decomposing shrimp bodies cause ammonia and nitrite to spike severely. Change 80–100% of the water or do a complete reset.

**Q: How long should I run activated carbon before replacing it?**  
A: During toxin treatment, replace carbon after 48–72 hours. Activated carbon becomes saturated and can release absorbed chemicals back into the water after 48 hours.

**Q: Is a sponge filter alone enough without water changes?**  
A: No. Sponge filters support oxygenation and beneficial bacteria but cannot remove chemicals. Water changes and activated carbon are mandatory in combination.

**Q: How long should I wait before adding new shrimp after treatment?**  
A: Wait at least 7–10 days after treatment is complete. Parameters must be stable for 3 consecutive days (NH3 = 0, NO2 = 0) before adding new shrimp.

## Summary — Treatment Protocol by Severity

```
🟡 MILD (perfume, cigarette smoke):
   → 30% water change + activated carbon 24h + increased aeration

🟠 MODERATE (air freshener, nail polish):
   → 50% water change × 2 times + activated carbon 48h + maximum sponge filter

🔴 SEVERE (mosquito spray, insecticide):
   → Net out shrimp immediately + continuous water changes + activated carbon 72h
   → Reassess at 24h: if deaths continue → complete tank reset
```

> **A note from real experience**: Prevention is always easier than treatment. A simple label on the tank — *"Live shrimp inside — no chemical sprays"* — can sometimes save your entire colony.
