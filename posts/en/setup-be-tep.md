# Aquarium Shrimp Setup

Setting up an aquarium shrimp tank correctly is the first and most important step to having a healthy and thriving shrimp tank. This article will guide you step by step in detail.

## Preparation Before Setup

### Choosing Tank Size

The appropriate tank size depends on:
- **Intended number of shrimp**
- **Available space**
- **Budget**

**Recommendations**:
- **Small tank (20-30 liters)**: Suitable for 10-20 small shrimp
- **Medium tank (40-60 liters)**: Ideal for beginners, easy to maintain
- **Large tank (80-120 liters)**: For experienced keepers, easier to stabilize environment

> **Note**: Larger tanks are usually easier to maintain because the water environment is more stable.

### Tank Placement

Choose an appropriate location:
- ✅ Avoid direct sunlight
- ✅ Avoid drafty areas
- ✅ Near electrical outlets
- ✅ Level, sturdy surface
- ✅ Easy to observe and maintain

## Required Equipment List

### 1. Tank and Cover

- Glass or acrylic tank
- Cover to prevent shrimp from jumping out
- Sturdy tank stand

### 2. Filtration System

**Common filter types**:

| Filter Type | Advantages | Disadvantages | Suitable For |
|-------------|-----------|---------------|--------------|
| **Sponge Filter** | Safe for baby shrimp, inexpensive | Limited capacity | Small tanks, breeding tanks |
| **Hang-on-Back (HOB)** | Easy to install, effective | May suck in baby shrimp | Medium tanks |
| **Canister Filter** | High capacity, quiet | Expensive, complex | Large tanks |

**Recommendation for beginners**: Sponge filter or HOB with intake guard.

### 3. Heating System

- **Heater**: 1W per liter of water
- **Thermometer**: To monitor temperature
- **Thermostat**: Automatically turns heater on/off

**Note**: If room temperature is always > 22°C, heater may not be needed.

### 4. Lighting System

- **LED Lights**: Energy-efficient, long lifespan
- **Wattage**: 0.5-1W per liter of water
- **Lighting time**: 6-8 hours/day (use automatic timer)

### 5. Air Pump

- Small air pump
- Airline tubing and air stone
- Important for high-density tanks

### 6. Substrate and Filter Media

- **Substrate**: Fine sand, small gravel, or specialized shrimp substrate
- **Filter Media**: Filter floss, ceramic rings, activated carbon

### 7. Supporting Tools

- Gravel vacuum
- Fish/shrimp net
- Water test kit (pH, NH3, NO2, NO3, GH, KH)
- Thermometer
- Timer for lights

## Step-by-Step Setup Process

### Step 1: Clean Tank and Equipment

1. Rinse tank with clean water (no soap)
2. Rinse substrate with water until water runs clear
3. Clean all equipment

### Step 2: Place Substrate

1. Add substrate to tank, 3-5 cm thick
2. Create slight slope toward back (creates depth)
3. Level the substrate surface

### Step 3: Install Equipment

1. **Install heater**:
   - Place in corner, fully submerged in water
   - Set temperature to 22-26°C

2. **Install filtration system**:
   - Install according to manufacturer's instructions
   - Ensure intake has guard

3. **Install lights**:
   - Place on tank cover or hang above
   - Connect to timer

4. **Install air pump**:
   - Place air stone in corner
   - Adjust air flow moderately

### Step 4: Add Water and Dechlorinate

1. **Pour water into tank**:
   - Pour gently to avoid disturbing substrate
   - Can place a plate or plastic bag to reduce water force

2. **Dechlorinate**:
   - Use dechlorinator (follow instructions)
   - Or let water sit for 24-48 hours

### Step 5: Decorate and Plant

1. **Place driftwood and rocks**:
   - Create hiding places for shrimp
   - Create aesthetic focal points

2. **Plant aquatic plants**:
   - Start with easy plants (Java Moss, Java Fern)
   - Plant into substrate or tie to driftwood
   - Leave open space for shrimp to swim

### Step 6: Run System and Cycle Tank

1. **Turn on all equipment**:
   - Filter, heater, lights, air pump
   - Check for normal operation

2. **Cycling process (nitrogen cycle)**:
   - **Week 1-2**: Beneficial bacteria start growing
   - **Week 2-4**: Nitrogen cycle completes
   - **Check**: NH3 and NO2 = 0, NO3 < 20 ppm

3. **Speed up cycling**:
   - Add beneficial bacteria (bottled bacteria)
   - Or add a little food to create NH3
   - Or use filter media from already cycled tank

### Step 7: Test and Adjust Water Parameters

Use test kit to check:

| Parameter | Target | How to Adjust |
|-----------|--------|---------------|
| **Temperature** | 22-26°C | Adjust heater |
| **pH** | 6.5-8.0 | Use pH up/down products |
| **GH** | 4-15 dGH | Add minerals or mix with RO water |
| **KH** | 2-8 dKH | Add baking soda or limestone |
| **NH3** | 0 ppm | Wait for cycling to complete |
| **NO2** | 0 ppm | Wait for cycling to complete |
| **NO3** | < 20 ppm | Water change |

### Step 8: Introduce Shrimp

**After cycling is complete** (NH3 = 0, NO2 = 0):

1. **Preparation**:
   - Turn off lights to reduce stress
   - Prepare water for acclimation

2. **Acclimation process**:
   - Float bag on water surface for 15-20 minutes (temperature equalization)
   - Open bag, add small amounts of tank water to bag (every 10 minutes)
   - After 30-40 minutes, gently release shrimp into tank

3. **Observation**:
   - Monitor shrimp for first 24 hours
   - Don't feed immediately, wait for shrimp to acclimate

## Maintenance Schedule After Setup

### Daily

- ✅ Check temperature
- ✅ Observe shrimp (health, behavior)
- ✅ Feed (if needed)

### Weekly

- ✅ Change 20-30% water
- ✅ Siphon substrate
- ✅ Test water parameters (NH3, NO2, NO3)
- ✅ Clean tank glass

### Monthly

- ✅ Clean filter (rinse filter floss with old tank water)
- ✅ Trim aquatic plants
- ✅ Check all equipment

## Common Setup Mistakes

### 1. Introducing Shrimp Too Early

**Problem**: Adding shrimp before cycling completes
**Consequence**: Shrimp die from toxic NH3 and NO2
**Solution**: Wait for cycling to complete, verify with test kit

### 2. Too Many Water Changes

**Problem**: Changing > 50% water at once
**Consequence**: Water shock, kills beneficial bacteria
**Solution**: Only change 20-30% at a time

### 3. Filter Too Strong

**Problem**: Suction too strong
**Consequence**: Sucks in baby shrimp, creates strong current
**Solution**: Reduce flow rate or add intake guard

### 4. Not Dechlorinating

**Problem**: Tap water contains harmful chlorine
**Consequence**: Shrimp die or stress
**Solution**: Always dechlorinate before adding water

### 5. Too Much Food

**Problem**: Overfeeding
**Consequence**: Water pollution, increased NH3
**Solution**: Feed moderately, shrimp can eat natural algae

## Conclusion

Setting up an aquarium shrimp tank correctly is the foundation for a healthy and thriving shrimp tank. Be patient during the cycling process and ensure water parameters are appropriate before introducing shrimp.

> **Final advice**: Don't rush! A carefully set up tank will save time and effort in maintenance later. Invest time in cycling the tank properly.

**Good luck with your aquarium shrimp tank!** 🦐
